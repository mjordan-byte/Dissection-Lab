About the heart

The heart is a fist-sized muscle with four chambers: the upper atria and lower ventricles. Between chambers, valves prevent backflow. Arteries carry blood away from the heart; veins carry it back.

Oxygen-poor blood enters the right atrium from the body via the vena cava, moves to the right ventricle, and travels through the pulmonary artery to the lungs. Oxygen-rich blood returns via the pulmonary vein to the left atrium, is pumped into the left ventricle, and exits through the aorta to the body.

The left ventricle wall is thicker than the right — it must pump blood the entire length of the body, while the right ventricle only reaches the lungs.

Orientation & identification
1

Place the heart in your tray. Imagine it inside a person facing you — their left is on your right. Lay the heart with the apex (pointed end) facing toward you, smooth rounded side up.

Find the diagonal groove of blood vessels — the interventricular sulcus. The half containing the entire apex is the left side.

2

Find the groove running diagonally across the surface — this contains the coronary artery that feeds blood to the heart muscle itself. Trace it as far as you can see it.

3

Find the ear-like flaps of darker tissue at the top — the auricles (one on each side). These extend from the atria and are part of the filling mechanism of the heart.

4

The front-most large vessel at the top is the pulmonary trunk. Just behind it is the aorta — slightly larger and with noticeably firmer, thicker walls. Place a probe in each to mark them. You may also see the brachiocephalic artery, a branch of the aorta.

5

Turn the heart to view its dorsal (back) side. Find the large opening near the right auricle — the superior vena cava. Below it is the inferior vena cava. Thread a finger from one to the other — both enter the right atrium.

6

On the dorsal side, on the left, find the openings of the pulmonary veins. They may be partly covered by fat. Place a pencil in the opening to confirm its position.

Opening the right side
a

Locate the pulmonary artery. Insert scissors and cut through the front of the vessel, continuing down through the right ventricle wall. Keep the cut diagonal above and parallel to the coronary artery. Stop at the end of the right ventricle cavity.

b

At the base of the pulmonary artery locate the pulmonary (semilunar) valve — three cup-shaped flaps that allow blood to flow outward only. No chordae tendineae are attached to this valve.

c

Inside the right ventricle find the tricuspid valve — three tissue leaflets connected by thin white chordae tendineae (heartstrings) anchored to the papillary muscles on the ventricle wall.

d

On the dorsal side, find the superior vena cava and cut down into the right atrium about 3 cm. Do not cut into the right ventricle. Thread a finger from the superior vena cava through the tricuspid valve — it should emerge in your right ventricle opening.

Q 8Can blood flow backwards from the right ventricle into the right atrium? Why or why not?
Opening the left side
e

Find the aorta and cut through it until you reach the aortic semilunar valve, then continue cutting down through the thick muscular wall of the left ventricle. This is a larger cut — go slowly and deliberately.

f

Make a similar incision on the left side to expose the left atrium, left ventricle, and the bicuspid (mitral) valve. Observe the chordae tendineae and papillary muscles here too.

g

Thread a finger or probe through the mitral valve from the left ventricle side — you should reach the left atrium. Locate the openings of the pulmonary veins entering the left atrium.

h

Insert a probe into the aorta and observe where it exits inside the heart. Find the aortic semilunar valve at the connection point — notice it has no chordae tendineae, unlike the mitral and tricuspid valves.

i

Using your ruler, measure the wall thickness of each ventricle at its thickest point. The difference should be very noticeable.
